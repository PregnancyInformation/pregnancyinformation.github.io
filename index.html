Understanding Placenta Previa

(Milky Goodness, 2024)

Pathology

Placenta previa is a complication during pregnancy that affects around 5.2 out of every 1,000 pregnancies. It is marked by the placenta being positioned in such a way that it either partially or fully obstructs the cervical opening. This condition can be categorized into four different types: low-lying, marginal, partial, and complete placenta previa (Jenabi et al., 2022).

Assessment

According to Anderson-Bagga and Sze (2023), gathering a patient's history is an essential step in assessing for placenta previa, as it helps to identify key risk factors associated with the condition. These include:

  • Advanced maternal age (over 35 years old)
  • Multiparity (having had multiple previous pregnancies)
  • A history of smoking or cocaine use
  • A history of curettage or previous caesarean sections

Observation of placenta previa’s key manifestations will help to guide diagnosis and treatment. A key sign of placenta previa is vaginal bleeding that is bright red and not accompanied by pain. This bleeding can become more severe as the pregnancy nears term, and the cervix starts to prepare for labor by effacing and dilating (Keenan-Lindsay, 2024).

Complications of Placenta Previa

The specific reason for placenta previa is still not clearly understood; however, it is linked to a variety of negative effects on both the mother and fetus. Some of these effects include:

  • Atypical placental attachment – an unusually located placenta
  • Bleeding occurring before or after birth
  • Abnormal positioning of the fetus
  • Limited fetal growth
  • Thrombophlebitis – inflammation of a vein caused by a clot (usually in the legs, it will be painful and swollen).
  • Early onset of labor
  • Heightened chance of infections, such as septicemia (Jenabi et al., 2022)

Diagnostic Tests

  • Routine ultrasound examination: Keenan-Lindsay (2024) mentioned that routine ultrasound examination can be used to identify low-lying placenta in early pregnancy.
  • Transabdominal and transvaginal ultrasound: If you have vaginal bleeding during your pregnancy, you'll first get an ultrasound on your belly (transabdominal ultrasound). If there's a concern about placenta previa, a more accurate and safe vaginal ultrasound (transvaginal ultrasound) will be performed to confirm the placenta's exact location (Anderson-Bagga and Sze, 2023).

(Ireland, 2014)

Nursing Interventions

Relevant Medical Interventions

  • Cesarean section: When placenta previa is diagnosed, a Cesarean section is the planned delivery method, usually scheduled between 36 and 37 weeks. However, an emergency Cesarean section will be performed earlier if the patient experiences excessive or constant bleeding. Ideally, a Cesarean section should be performed under controlled conditions to ensure the best outcome (Anderson-Bagga and Sze, 2023).
  • Blood transfusion: Excessive bleeding can occur in placenta previa, and blood transfusions are a crucial intervention to manage blood loss. In case of significant bleeding, a patient should be prepared for a potential blood transfusion by having two large intravenous lines inserted and 2 to 4 units of blood "crossed and matched" and on standby. Regional anesthesia is considered the best option for delivery because it leads to less operative blood loss, reducing the need for a transfusion (Anderson-Bagga and Sze, 2023).

Pharmacology

Pharmacological medications are used to treat the condition, going from least to most severe.

  • Iron supplements and Folic Acid: to treat or prevent anemia from blood loss during bleeding episodes.
  • Corticosteroids: to accelerate fetal lung maturity if early delivery is anticipated. The goal is to maintain the pregnancy until the fetal lungs are mature enough to decrease the risk of respiration distress (Watts, 2024).
  • Tocolytics: may be considered in cases of mild bleeding and uterine contractions to delay labour. The primary goal of tocolytic here is to stop contractions long enough to enable one or two doses of glucocorticosteroids to work and help mature the baby’s lungs, usually 24 to 48 hours prior to birth (Watts, 2024). However, because tocolytic drugs can have adverse effects, the nurse must closely monitor both the mother and fetus during treatment (Watts, 2024).
  • Blood Products/transfusion: in cases of severe bleeding. Placenta previa can cause sudden and heavy bleeding (hemorrhage), which may lead to anemia or hypovolemic shock. To manage severe anemia or significant blood loss (Watts, 2024).

Determinants of Health

The patient's education and social environment can significantly affect their care. For example, a patient who struggles with medical terminology and relies on online forums for information requires a tailored approach.

  • Education and literacy: Use simple, clear language and avoid medical jargon. Confirm comprehension using the teach-back method and provide accessible learning tools. Provide information in multiple formats that are adapted to different literacy levels, languages, and cultural contexts, including offering interpreter services when individuals do not speak the primary language. These strategies empower individuals to better understand their conditions and follow care plans safely. This helps individuals understand information to stay healthy (Keenan-Lindsay, 2024).
  • Social environment: Build trust through empathy and non-judgmental, open communication to create a safe environment where individuals feel heard and respected. It is important to acknowledge and respect individua’s unique cultural backgrounds, values, and experiences. Correct misinformation respectfully and connect individuals to reliable community support (Keenan-Lindsay, 2024). This empowers individuals to make safe, informed decisions and reduces the risks posed by unregulated online sources.

Priority Teaching for Patients

  • Instruct the patient to do left side lying and to use a pillow on their right hip when lying on their back.
  • Contact the doctor for any amount of bleeding, especially if it is bright red.
  • Bed rest is important. Avoid sexual intercourse, tampons, or heavy lifting (Keenan-Lindsay, 2024).

Relevant Resources

When dealing with a condition like placenta previa, having access to accurate and reliable resources is crucial for patient education and support. Here are some relevant resources for patients and their families:

  • The Society of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists of Canada (SOGC): The SOGC provides information on complications like placenta previa. You can visit their website for more details: SOGC Placenta Previa Information (Keenan-Lindsay, 2024).
  • Maternal-Fetal Medicine Specialists: Having a direct relationship with a Maternal–Fetal Medicine Specialist is essential for managing a high-risk pregnancy.
  • Community support groups:
