Understanding Placenta Previa

Pathology

Placenta previa is a complication during pregnancy that affects around 5.2 out of every 1,000 pregnancies. It is marked by the placenta being positioned in such a way that it either partially or fully obstructs the cervical opening. This condition can be categorized into four different types: low-lying, marginal, partial, and complete placenta previa (Jenabi et al., 2022).

Reference: Jenabi, E., Salimi, Z., Bashirian, S., Khazaei, S., & Ayubi, E. (2022). The risk factors associated with placenta previa: An umbrella review. Placenta, 117, 21-27.

Assessment

According to Anderson-Bagga and Sze (2023), gathering a patient's history is an essential step in assessing for placenta previa, as it helps to identify key risk factors associated with the condition. These include:

  • Advanced maternal age (over 35 years old)
  • Multiparity (having had multiple previous pregnancies)
  • A history of smoking or cocaine use
  • A history of curettage or previous caesarean sections

Reference: Anderson-Bagga, F. M., & Sze, A. (2023, June 12). Placenta previa. Europe PMC.

Observation of placenta previa’s key manifestations will help to guide diagnosis and treatment. A key sign of placenta previa is vaginal bleeding that is bright red and not accompanied by pain. This bleeding can become more severe as the pregnancy nears term, and the cervix starts to prepare for labor by effacing and dilating (Keenan-Lindsay, 2024).

Reference: Keenan-Lindsay, L. (2024). Leifer’s introduction to maternity and pediatric nursing in Canada (2nd ed). Elsevier.

Complications of Placenta Previa

The specific reason for placenta previa is still not clearly understood; however, it is linked to a variety of negative effects on both the mother and fetus. Some of these effects include:

  • Atypical placental attachment
  • Bleeding occurring before or after birth
  • Abnormal positioning of the fetus
  • Limited fetal growth
  • Thrombophlebitis
  • Early onset of labor
  • Heightened chance of infections, such as septicemia (Jenabi et al., 2022)

Reference: Jenabi, E., Salimi, Z., Bashirian, S., Khazaei, S., & Ayubi, E. (2022). The risk factors associated with placenta previa: An umbrella review. Placenta, 117, 21-27.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Routine ultrasound examination: Keenan-Lindsay (2024) mentioned that routine ultrasound examination can be used to identify low-lying placenta in early pregnancy.
  • Transabdominal and transvaginal ultrasound: If you have vaginal bleeding during your pregnancy, you'll first get an ultrasound on your belly (transabdominal ultrasound). If there's a concern about placenta previa, a more accurate and safe vaginal ultrasound (transvaginal ultrasound) will be performed to confirm the placenta's exact location (Anderson-Bagga and Sze, 2023).

References: Keenan-Lindsay, L. (2024). Leifer’s introduction to maternity and pediatric nursing in Canada (2nd ed). Elsevier. | Anderson-Bagga, F. M., & Sze, A. (2023, June 12). Placenta previa. Europe PMC.

Nursing Interventions

When placenta previa is present, close monitoring of both the mother and baby is essential. This allows nurses to promptly identify any changes and ensure their well-being.

  • Continuous monitoring of the fetal heart rate and uterine contractions (Keenan-Lindsay, 2024). This will allow nurses to detect signs of any fetal distress and identify any changes in the mother’s condition.
  • Nurses have a crucial role in monitoring and reporting any vaginal bleeding, along with documenting signs of shock. During active bleeding, they must check a patient's vital signs every 15 minutes (Keenan-Lindsay, 2024).

Reference: Keenan-Lindsay, L. (2024). Leifer’s introduction to maternity and pediatric nursing in Canada (2nd ed). Elsevier.

Relevant Medical Interventions

  • Cesarean section: When placenta previa is diagnosed, a Cesarean section is the planned delivery method, usually scheduled between 36 and 37 weeks. However, an emergency Cesarean section will be performed earlier if the patient experiences excessive or constant bleeding.
  • Blood transfusion: Excessive bleeding can occur in placenta previa, and blood transfusions are a crucial intervention to manage blood loss. In case of significant bleeding, a patient should be prepared for a potential blood transfusion by having two large intravenous lines inserted and 2 to 4 units of blood "crossed and matched" and on standby.

Reference: Anderson-Bagga, F. M., & Sze, A. (2023, June 12). Placenta previa. Europe PMC.

Pharmacology

Pharmacological medications are used to treat the condition, going from least to most severe.

  • Iron supplements and Folic Acid: to treat or prevent anemia from blood loss during bleeding episodes.
  • Corticosteroids: to accelerate fetal lung maturity if early delivery is anticipated.
  • Tocolytics: may be considered in cases of mild bleeding and uterine contractions to delay labour.
  • Blood Products/transfusion: in cases of severe bleeding.

Reference: Watts, N. (2020). Nursing care of patients with complications during pregnancy. In L. Keenan-Lindsay. (Ed.), Leifer’s introduction to maternity and pediatric nursing in Canada (1st ed., pp. 420-549). Elsevier.

Determinants of Health

The patient's education and social environment can significantly affect their care. For example, a patient like Brittany who struggles with medical terminology and relies on online forums for information requires a tailored approach.

  • Education and literacy: Use simple, clear language and avoid medical jargon. Confirm comprehension using the teach-back method and provide accessible learning tools.
  • Social environment: Build trust through empathy and non-judgmental communication. Correct misinformation respectfully and connect the patient to reliable community support.

Reference: Keenan-Lindsay, L. (2020). Overview of Perinatal and pediatric nursing in Canada. In L. Keenan-Lindsay. (Ed.), Leifer’s introduction to maternity and pediatric nursing in Canada (1st ed., pp. 52-122). Elsevier.

Priority Teaching for Patients

  • Instruct the patient to do left side lying and to use a pillow on their right hip when lying on their back.
  • Contact the doctor for any amount of bleeding, especially if it is bright red.
  • Bed rest is important. Avoid sexual intercourse, tampons, or heavy lifting.

Reference: Keenan-Lindsay, L., & Leifer, G., (2023). Leifer's introduction to maternity and pediatric nursing in Canada . (2nd Ed.). Elsevier.
